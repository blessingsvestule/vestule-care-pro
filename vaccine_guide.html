Nigerian Baby Vaccine Guide

What every Nigerian working mum needs to know about her baby's immunization — from missed dates to reactions and when to call the doctor.

📋
Source of all information on this page:
All vaccine schedule information is sourced from the National Programme on Immunization (NPI) 2025–2026 schedule published by the National Primary Health Care Development Agency (NPHCDA) of Nigeria, and catch-up guidance is based on World Health Organization (WHO) immunization protocols.

This page does not constitute medical advice. Always consult your paediatrician or nearest government health centre for decisions about your baby's health.

If you miss a vaccine date

Missing a date does not mean starting over. Here is exactly what to do depending on how long ago you missed it.

How long missed What to do
Less than 4 weeks Go to the clinic as soon as possible. The vaccine can still be given with no restart needed.
4 to 12 weeks Still go. A healthcare worker will advise on a catch-up plan. Do not skip — catching up is always better than not vaccinating at all.
More than 3 months Visit your paediatrician or nearest Primary Health Centre (PHC). Explain the situation — a new schedule will be worked out for your baby.
Vaccine was out of stock Call ahead to confirm availability before your next trip. Ask the clinic to notify you when it is restocked. Do not delay once it is available.
Baby was ill on the day Wait until baby recovers — usually 2 to 5 days. Most mild illnesses are not a barrier to vaccination. Reschedule as soon as baby is well.
💡
Important — do not restart the series

According to WHO immunization guidelines, if a baby misses a dose in a vaccine series — such as Pentavalent or PCV — the series does not need to restart from the beginning. The missed dose is simply given as soon as possible and the remaining doses continue from there.

This applies to all multi-dose vaccines on the Nigerian NPI schedule.

Vaccine reactions — what is normal and what is not

Some reactions after vaccination are completely normal and expected. Others need attention. Here is how to tell the difference.

Normal — no action needed
Mild fever (below 38.5°C) · Redness, swelling or tenderness at the injection site · Fussiness or crying for 1 to 2 days · Sleepiness or loss of appetite · Small hard lump at injection site that may last weeks

These are signs that your baby's immune system is responding to the vaccine. They usually resolve within 1 to 3 days without treatment.
Watch closely — call your doctor if it continues
Fever above 38.5°C · Crying that lasts more than 3 hours and cannot be soothed · Swelling larger than 5cm at injection site · Unusual drowsiness or difficulty waking

These reactions are uncommon but can occur. Monitor your baby closely. If the symptom does not improve within 24 hours or gets worse — contact your paediatrician.
Urgent — go to hospital immediately
High fever above 40°C · Difficulty breathing or wheezing · Swelling of the face, lips or throat · Pale, limp or unresponsive · Seizures or convulsions · Rash spreading across the body

These reactions are very rare but require immediate medical attention. Go to your nearest hospital emergency immediately — do not wait.
💊
Managing mild fever at home

For mild fever after vaccination, paracetamol drops (such as Emzolyn or Tempol) at the correct dose for your baby's weight are safe and effective.

Do not give aspirin to babies. Do not give ibuprofen to babies under 3 months. Always follow the dosage on the packaging or as prescribed by your doctor.

Keep your baby cool, offer frequent feeds, and monitor their temperature. Most fevers after vaccination resolve within 24 to 48 hours.

Before and after the clinic visit

A few simple steps make the appointment smoother for you and your baby.

✅
Before the appointment
1
Confirm the clinic is open and the vaccines are in stock — call ahead to save a wasted trip
2
Bring your baby's vaccination card — this is your official government record and must accompany your baby to every visit
3
Bring a change of clothes for your baby — some babies have loose stools after oral vaccines
4
Feed your baby before the appointment — a hungry baby is more distressed
5
Allow 1 to 2 hours — especially at the 6, 10, and 14-week appointments where multiple vaccines are given
🏠
After the appointment
1
Confirm the nurse has recorded the vaccine in your baby's vaccination card before you leave
2
Ask for the next appointment date and add it to your MamaShield Google Calendar before leaving the clinic
3
Monitor your baby for 15 to 30 minutes at the clinic after injection before going home
4
Keep the injection site clean and dry — do not rub or massage it
5
Update your MamaShield tracker — tap ✓ on the vaccines given today

Are vaccines really free in Nigeria?

🇳🇬
Yes — all routine NPI vaccines are free

Under the Nigerian National Programme on Immunization (NPI), all routine childhood vaccines are provided free of charge at government-owned Primary Health Centres (PHCs), General Hospitals, and Teaching Hospitals across Nigeria.
